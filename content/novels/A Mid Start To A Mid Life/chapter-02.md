Strolling through the beautiful scenery of our cultivation sect should be enlightening.

Instead...

Bro, I'm bored as hell.

Come on.

This is supposed to be the cultivation world.

The strong devour the weak. Everyone should be desperately chasing opportunities, murdering each other over a single spirit herb, and searching for the inheritance cave of some ancient senior who died ten thousand years ago after conveniently sealing all of his techniques inside a glowing jade.

But no.

Nothing.

Just peace and quiet.

Everyone behaves like civilized human beings.

The disciples sit on their ridiculously expensive jade beds, close their eyes, circulate their qi through their meridians, contemplate the cycle of heaven and earth, and cultivate like good little boys and girls.

It's honestly disappointing.

Our sect even has an entire War Peak.

Do you know when the last war was?

About two hundred years ago.

Two.

Hundred.

Years.

There isn't even a Holy Maiden.

No Holy Son.

No peerless beauty whose smile topples kingdoms.

No cold, aloof genius with countless admirers.

Not even the Sect Master's daughter secretly longing for freedom.

The Sect Master himself is... nice.

Actually nice.

He listens to his disciples, takes care of the sect, has been happily married for over fifty years, and has two children.

What happened to all the clichés?

The entire sect basically runs like a university.

For your first ten years, your education is fully funded.

If your cultivation talent turns out to be exceptional, congratulations—you earn scholarships and additional resources.

If you're average...

Well, welcome to the workforce.

The sect assigns you a job, deducts your tuition from your salary, and after completing another ten years of mandatory service, you're free to leave.

Then you can return to the mortal world, establish your own cultivation clan, join another sect, or do whatever you want.

See?

It's literally college.

Except your professors can cut mountains in half.

I sighed.

Maybe I was just unlucky.

A perfectly average disciple.

Living in a perfectly average sect.

During a perfectly average era.

With perfectly average talent.

Honestly...

It was starting to piss me off.

Still, I couldn't deny one thing.

The sect was beautiful.

Towering ancient trees lined the stone pathways.

Gardens bloomed in every direction.

Streams wound through parks filled with pavilions.

There were bathhouses every kilometer, comfortable dormitories with surprisingly decent living conditions, dining halls for mortal disciples, and even shopping streets where cultivators could buy luxury wine, fancy robes, and all sorts of unnecessary things.

Seriously.

Why is this place so functional?

Now you're probably wondering...

_"Xu Huang, if life here is so great, what's your problem?"_

Simple.

There's no such thing as a free lunch.

"...Hey, Xu!"

I turned around.

A young man nearly a head taller than me walked over, his green disciple robes fluttering in the breeze. At around one hundred ninety centimeters, Zhou looked exactly like the sort of supporting character you'd expect in a cultivation novel.

Handsome.

Tall.

Long black hair.

A sword hanging from his belt.

Not protagonist-level handsome.

More like _the protagonist's reliable friend_ handsome.

"Hey, Zhou."

I stopped walking and waited for him to catch up.

When he reached me, he raised a fist.

I bumped it.

We were bros.

"So," I asked, "what are you up to?"

"Checking out girls."

"...Really?"

"Of course. Why else would I take a break from cultivating?"

"You could've said you were stabilizing your cultivation realm or comprehending the Dao."

"Why would I lie?"

"I don't know... to sound dignified?"

He snorted.

"We're brothers. If I can't honestly talk about girls with you, then who am I supposed to talk to?"

"...Fair point."

"So," I asked, "besides appreciating the scenery, what are you actually doing?"

"An elder from Smithing Peak is giving a lecture on the finer points of qi circulation during the Qi Gathering Realm."

He shrugged.

"Figured I'd listen in."

I nodded.

"Sure."

I didn't have anything better to do anyway.
